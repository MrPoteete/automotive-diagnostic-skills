## Fuel System — Lean Conditions

### Bilateral Lean Fuel Trim (P0171 + P0174)

**Confirmed Root Cause**: EVAP purge valve stuck open (confirmed on GM 3.6L — defective *replacement* valve)
**Source**: `[T1: confirmed-case:2026-0408-traverse]` | Vault: `patterns/bilateral-lean-fuel-trim`, `dtcs/P0171`, `dtcs/P0174`, `components/evap-purge-valve`

**Presentation**:
- P0171 AND P0174 stored together on a V6 or V8
- Both banks showing elevated positive LTFT simultaneously
- May follow a prior purge valve replacement (replacement part can be defective)

**Key Evidence** (order of diagnosis):
1. **Purge valve unplug test**: Unplug connector at idle, watch STFT on both banks for 30–60 sec. STFT moving toward 0% = valve stuck open.
2. **Scan tool command**: If bidirectional control available — command valve closed and observe fuel trim response.
3. **Smoke test** (if purge valve not source): post-MAF intake leak survey with purge valve disconnected.
4. **MAF g/s** at warm idle vs. spec (contamination or under-reporting).
5. **Fuel pressure** at rail (rare as bilateral cause, but eliminates it).

**Differentiators**:

| Finding | Points To |
|---|---|
| Both banks lean, STFT improves after unplug | Purge valve stuck open — replace |
| Both banks lean, no STFT change after unplug | Vacuum leak, MAF, or fuel pressure |
| Lean worse at idle, better at cruise | Vacuum leak (unmetered air proportionally larger at idle) |
| Lean worse at load, better at idle | MAF under-reporting or low fuel pressure |
| Recently replaced purge valve, codes returned | Defective replacement — test and replace again |
| Only one bank lean (P0171 or P0174, not both) | Bank-specific cause: O2 sensor, bank vacuum leak, injector |

**Critical Warning**: Do not replace O2 sensors on bilateral lean — sensors are reporting correctly. Do not assume a recently replaced purge valve is good.

**Vehicles**: GM 3.6L Lambda platform confirmed (2009–2017 Traverse, Acadia, Enclave); pattern likely applicable to any V6/V8 with EVAP purge to intake manifold.

## HVAC / Air Conditioning

### AC Warm — Compressor Clutch Not Engaging (BMW IHKA Systems)

**Confirmed Root Cause**: Shorted AC compressor clutch coil — internal winding failure (0 ohm, spec 3–5 ohm)
**Source**: `[T1: confirmed-case:2026-0408-bmw-328d]` | Vault: `patterns/ac-warm-clutch-not-engaging`, `dtcs/BMW-80436E`, `components/ac-clutch-coil`, `vehicles/2015-bmw-f30-328d`

**Presentation**:
- AC blows warm regardless of mode/temperature setting
- Compressor clutch not engaging (no click, center of pulley not spinning)
- BMW IHKA fault 80436E stored: "Compressor Clutch: Break in wiring or short to B+"
- Equalized static pressures (e.g., 100/100 psi) — confirms compressor not running
- System may also be undercharged

**Key Evidence** (order of diagnosis):
1. **Static pressure equalization**: Both sides equalized = compressor 100% not running.
2. **Refrigerant charge**: Recover and weigh. Low charge — recharge to spec and retest (pressure switch may be blocking command).
3. **IHKA command check**: Harness disconnected, engine running — full voltage on clutch wire. Command AC on/off, watch for voltage change (~9.6V when IHKA backs off PWM) = IHKA alive and commanding.
4. **Clutch coil resistance**: Disconnect harness, measure across coil. 0 ohm = dead short = replace compressor.
5. **Belt-off mechanical**: Spin clutch disc (should be free), spin pulley (bearing check).

**BMW IHKA Electrical Facts** (critical — frequently misread):
- ~9.6V on clutch wire with engine running and AC off = NORMAL monitoring voltage — not a fault
- ~9V on clutch wire with ignition OFF = CAN bus capacitance bleed-down — drops to 0V in 3–5 min — NOT a wiring short
- Voltage drops when IHKA commands clutch = normal PWM modulation = IHKA working correctly

**Differentiators**:

| Finding | Points To |
|---|---|
| 0 ohm coil resistance | Internal winding short — replace compressor |
| Coil OL (open) | Broken winding or harness break — inspect first |
| Good coil + no voltage at clutch | Control module fault, pressure switch open, or relay |
| Good coil + voltage + clutch will not engage | Mechanical: worn air gap, seized compressor |
| 9.6V on clutch wire, engine running | Normal IHKA monitoring — not a fault |
| 9V with ignition off | CAN bus capacitance — wait 3–5 min, it will drop |

**Requires**: BMW-capable scan tool (ISTA, Carly, or equivalent) — generic OBD readers will not display IHKA codes.

**Vehicles**: 2015 BMW 328d xDrive (F30, B47 diesel) confirmed. BMW IHKA monitoring voltage behavior likely consistent across F-series platforms.

### AC Warm — TXV Stuck Closed (Thermostatic Element Failure or Moisture Ice Blockage)

**Confirmed Root Cause**: Thermostatic Expansion Valve restricted or blocked — mechanical thermostatic element failure or moisture ice blockage at orifice
**Source**: `[T1: confirmed-case:2016-honda-crv-txv]` | Vault: `components/txv`

**Presentation**:
- AC blows cool (not cold) initially, often only one side of cabin
- After 2–5 minutes of operation: blows warm from all vents
- System works briefly after vehicle has sat for 10+ minutes (key indicator)
- Compressor IS running (differentiates from clutch-not-engaging pattern above)

**Key Evidence** (order of diagnosis):
1. **Static pressure split**: High side normal (~80–110 psi at ambient), low side critically low (at or below 15 psi). Pressures fail to equalize after 10+ min with system off. Normal systems equalize to the same value.
2. **Running low side vacuum**: Low side drops to near-zero or vacuum within minutes of operation.
3. **High side spike**: High side climbs excessively (250–400+ psi) while low side is at vacuum.

**"Works After Soak" Mechanism**:
- TXV thermostatic bulb senses evaporator outlet temperature
- After 10+ min off: evaporator warms to ambient — bulb reads warm — TXV partially opens — brief cooling
- After minutes running: evaporator chills — bulb reads cold — failed TXV snaps shut — low side goes to vacuum — warm air
- Moisture freeze variant: same pattern; ice melts during soak, refreezes once evaporator drops to ~32 deg F

**Differentiators**:

| Finding | Points To |
|---|---|
| Static split (110 high / 12 low) after 10+ min | TXV or liquid line restriction |
| Both sides equalize to same LOW value | Low charge, no restriction (different problem) |
| System 25%+ low AND restriction present | Moisture freeze more probable |
| Works after soak, fails after 2–5 min | TXV element failure OR moisture freeze (identical presentation) |
| High side LOW and low side elevated at static | Compressor internal bypass (opposite pattern) |

**Repair**:
1. Recover refrigerant (weigh out)
2. Replace TXV
3. Replace receiver-drier (mandatory when high side opened — addresses moisture)
4. Evacuate to at or below 500 microns, hold 45 minutes minimum
5. Recharge to factory spec weight (verify underhood decal)

**Not applicable to orifice tube systems** (common on domestic Ford/GM vehicles).

**Vehicles**: Any R-134a or R-1234yf TXV system. 2016 Honda CR-V confirmed.
