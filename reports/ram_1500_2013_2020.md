## Executive Summary

Electronic power steering failure is the dominant complaint driver for the 2013–2020 Ram 1500, accounting for 1,844 steering complaints — 20.5% of the total 9,013 NHTSA filings — with an active ODI investigation (RQ23003) flagging it as a pre-recall watchlist item. Engine, powertrain, and fuel system failures follow at significant volume, supported by 588 TSBs and amplified by 65 open NHTSA recalls plus 10 Transport Canada recalls covering airbags, ESC software, diesel fuel pumps, and accelerator pedal entrapment. Any Ram 1500 coming through the door warrants a VIN recall check before diagnosis begins — covered repairs are customer-zero-cost and must not be missed.

## Failure Pattern Analysis

Derived from keyword analysis of complaint narratives for the top failure system.

### Steering
**196 complaints mention this pattern (98.0%)**

**What it is:** Complete or intermittent Electronic Power Steering (EPS) failure — the system drops assist without warning, often at low speed during a turn. This is the single highest-volume complaint category in the dataset and the subject of an open ODI investigation (RQ23003, 380+ complaints on file). Do not treat this as a routine steering rack replacement until EPS control module, wiring, and fuse integrity are confirmed.

**How it presents:** Customer reports a red steering wheel icon on the dash, sudden heavy steering effort, or complete loss of power assist — most commonly during parking maneuvers or low-speed turns. Some units require multiple key cycles or extended warm-up before assist returns, pointing to thermal or voltage sensitivity in the EPS module.

**What to look for:** Pull C-codes first (see DTC table — C1277, C1441, C1442, C1443 are high-priority). Inspect the 120A battery cable fuse and the EPS fuse in the underhood box — owners are reporting DIY fuse replacements, which may mask the root cause. Check for corrosion at EPS module connectors and verify battery/charging system health before condemning the rack or motor. Cross-reference NHTSA investigation RQ23003 for developing repair guidance.

Representative complaint excerpts:
> *"Power steering went out when I try to turn on car I get a red steering wheel with..."*
> *"The steering wheel warning lamp turned on suddenly when I went to turn into my neighborhood and could not turn the wheel"*
> *"The EPS (electronic power steering) has failed completely, making the pickup truck nearly impossible to steer at slower speed"*

### Electrical / Instrument Cluster
**79 complaints mention this pattern (39.5%)**

**What it is:** Instrument cluster warning lamp activity directly associated with EPS events — specifically the red steering column icon with exclamation point. This pattern is frequently co-occurring with the primary steering failure and in many cases the cluster is the first indicator of an underlying EPS fault, not an independent electrical problem.

**How it presents:** Intermittent flashing of the steering warning lamp, sometimes accompanied by power steering disengagement. The complaint data shows some units cycle in and out of assist based on temperature — assist returns after extended idle or repeated key cycles. When no warning lights illuminate during a loss-of-assist event, suspect a sensor or communication fault rather than a hard electrical failure.

**What to look for:** Treat active steering warning lamps as an EPS system fault until proven otherwise. Use a capable scan tool (wiTECH or equivalent) to pull all stored and pending C-codes — intermittent faults may not be present at write-in. If the cluster is throwing warnings without corresponding DTCs, inspect the CAN bus and body control module (BCM) for communication errors. Do not replace the cluster as a first step.

Representative complaint excerpts:
> *"I'm currently experiencing an intermittent flashing red steering column and exclamation point on my dash as well as the power steering disengaging, forcing me to manually..."*
> *"The power steering rack works sometimes and sometimes I have to turn my truck on for at least 20 to 30 minutes until the truck heats up..."*
> *"No warning lights were illuminated"*

### Check Engine / MIL Light
**66 complaints mention this pattern (33.0%)**

**What it is:** MIL illumination appearing in the context of steering-related complaints, indicating cross-system fault activity. In this dataset, MIL events are co-reported alongside EPS failures, suggesting either a shared root cause (charging system, battery voltage) or a secondary fault triggered by EPS module errors propagating across the CAN network.

**How it presents:** Customer presents with a check engine light alongside a steering complaint. High failure mileages reported (58K–200K range) suggest this pattern spans both early wear and extended-use failures — not limited to a single mileage window.

**What to look for:** Pull all modules, not just the PCM. An EPS fault can set codes in multiple control units simultaneously. Prioritize the steering system diagnosis before chasing engine codes — confirm the MIL is not a downstream effect of a voltage event caused by EPS module behavior. If P-codes are present alongside C-codes, address the C-codes first.

### Stalling / Loss of Power
**27 complaints mention this pattern (13.5%)**

**What it is:** Sudden loss of EPS assist characterized by customers as "loss of power" or a stalling sensation — not engine stall in most cases, but the abrupt removal of steering assist creating a perceived vehicle behavior change. A subset of these complaints involve actual electrical intervention by the owner (fuse replacement on the battery cable), confirming a high-current fault path in the EPS circuit.

**How it presents:** Customer describes the truck suddenly becoming hard to steer, sometimes conflating it with an engine or drivetrain event. The DIY 120A battery cable fuse installation noted in complaints is a field indicator that the EPS system drew excessive current, blowing the OEM protection device.

**What to look for:** If a customer has already replaced fuses or installed inline fuse holders, the EPS motor or module has likely drawn an overcurrent event. Inspect the EPS motor for binding or internal short before replacing fuses and returning the vehicle. Verify battery cable condition at both ends — high-resistance connections can cause voltage drops that trigger EPS shutdowns.

Representative complaint excerpts:
> *"Sudden loss of power steering"*
> *"Loss of power steering"*
> *"I have purchased and installed a 10 amp fuse in the fuse panel and a 120 amp battery cable fuse on the battery"*

### Brakes
**11 complaints mention this pattern (5.5%)**

**What it is:** Brake system complaints co-occurring with EPS failures — primarily ABS warning light illumination and repeated brake system malfunctions. A Transport Canada recall (2024503) covers a software defect in the ESC system on 2019 models that can deactivate stability control without warning, which is directly relevant here.

**How it presents:** ABS warning light illumination during or after an EPS event; brake system failures (pads, calipers, fluid) recurring within short intervals; "Service Power Steering" warning preceding a curb impact. The intersection of steering and brake complaints in the same incident suggests loss-of-control events are occurring.

**What to look for:** On 2019 models, confirm ESC recall 2024503 (Transport Canada) has been completed. For repeated brake hardware failures, inspect for driver overcorrection due to loss of EPS assist — the brake system may be collateral damage, not the primary failure. Pull ABS/ESC codes alongside steering codes on any unit presenting with both systems flagged.

Representative complaint excerpts:
> *"The contact stated that while her husband was driving at an undisclosed speed, the ABS warning light illuminated"*
> *"The brake system (pads, calipers, hydraulic fluid) failed three times in less than 90 days"*
> *"The 'service power steering' warning light did illuminate when the power steering failed and the initial impact with the curb occurred"*

### Engine Misfires / Rough Running
**8 complaints mention this pattern (4.0%)**

**What it is:** Rough running and vibration complaints appearing alongside EPS-related events. In this complaint subset, the vibration is primarily felt through the steering wheel and pedals, suggesting the root cause may still be EPS or driveline related rather than a classic ignition misfire pattern.

**How it presents:** Customer describes steering wheel vibration and rough feel through the pedals — often reported after returning from another shop. The $2,700+ repair quote noted in complaints indicates EPS rack-and-pinion replacement is being recommended, which may or may not be warranted without thorough diagnostics.

**What to look for:** Do not assume misfire without confirming P030X codes from the PCM. Steering wheel vibration on this platform is more commonly tied to EPS motor cogging, driveshaft imbalance, or tire/wheel issues than to engine misfire. Verify with a road force balance and driveshaft runout check before engine diagnosis. Reference the 2026 TSB on camshaft/rocker arm replacement for 3.6L engines if rough running is confirmed independent of steering.

Representative complaint excerpts:
> *"I also noticed a steering wheel light with an exclamation running through it on my instrument panel"*
> *"After receiving my 2013 Ram 1500 from the shop I began to experience heavy vibrations felt through the steering wheel and pedals"*
> *"Complete loss of power steering while driving — significant cost of repairs quoted to me (over $2,700 for parts and labor)"*

### HVAC / AC
**2 complaints mention this pattern (1.0%)**

**What it is:** Two complaints reference HVAC conditions in the context of EPS behavior — specifically warm-up time required before power steering assist engages. This is almost certainly a thermal sensitivity issue within the EPS module or motor, not an HVAC failure.

**How it presents:** Customer reports power steering only works after the truck warms up, which they associate with running the heater or AC. The actual mechanism is likely EPS module thermal recovery or a temperature-dependent voltage threshold.

**What to look for:** Do not diagnose as an HVAC fault. If assist is temperature-dependent, focus on EPS module connector integrity, cold-soak voltage testing of the EPS circuit, and any TSBs addressing EPS thermal performance. Document ambient temperature and soak time at write-in.
