## Executive Summary

Engine failure — primarily catastrophic internal damage driven by oil consumption and lubrication loss — accounts for 35.2% of all complaints across this generation and represents the single highest-risk failure mode you will encounter on these vehicles. With 3,405 NHTSA complaints, 180 TSBs, and **17 open recall campaigns** (plus 9 Canadian cross-border recalls), this model range carries an unusually heavy service burden that spans powertrain, brakes, steering, and fuel delivery. Before touching any Sorento that rolls into your bay, run the VIN — recall exposure is broad, and multiple campaigns address safety-critical systems with no-charge repair obligations.

## Failure Pattern Analysis

### Check Engine / MIL Light
**115 complaints mention this pattern (57.5%)**

**What it is:** The MIL is the most common entry point for engine-related complaints on this platform, but on the Sorento it frequently signals something more serious than a routine emissions fault. A high proportion of these complaints escalate to catastrophic engine failure — the MIL was either the first warning or, critically, failed to illuminate at all until mechanical damage was already done.

**How it presents:** Customer comes in with an active MIL, possible knock or ticking, and may report recent oil changes with no noted consumption issues. In many documented cases, the light appeared alongside or just before a complete engine failure event. A significant subset of complaints note the MIL activating with no prior symptoms, then the engine failing shortly after.

**What to look for:** Pull all stored and pending codes immediately — do not clear without documentation. Check oil level and condition on every MIL visit regardless of stated complaint. If the engine has any audible knock or tick accompanying the MIL, treat it as a pre-failure condition. Cross-reference the VIN against the active engine-related TSBs (SC340, WTY035) and recall campaigns before ordering parts. The Theta II 2.4L GDI is the primary offender; ECM logic improvement campaigns are active as of late 2025.

Representative complaint excerpts:
> *"The vehicle developed progressive engine failure symptoms including: Engine knocking and abnormal mechanical noise Loss of power while driving Misfire conditions Excessive oil consumption Progressive..."*
> *"The failure mileage was approximately 134,000"*
> *"I just now hit 100k miles no reason for my motor to be blowing please put the recall back on for the 2015 Kia's"*

### Stalling / Loss of Power
**62 complaints mention this pattern (31.0%)**

**What it is:** Sudden loss of motive power, typically at highway speed, with simultaneous loss of power steering and power brakes. This is a safety-critical failure mode — it leaves the driver with a dead vehicle in traffic with degraded control authority. Many of these events trace back to catastrophic lubrication failure, not an ignition or fuel delivery fault.

**How it presents:** Customer reports the vehicle abruptly dying at speed, often preceded by a loud knock or rattle from the engine. No prior warning lights in many cases. Power steering goes heavy, brake pedal goes hard, and the vehicle coasts to a stop. Some customers describe it as the engine and everything electrical shutting off simultaneously.

**What to look for:** Confirm whether this is a true stall (engine died) or a loss of drive (engine running, no power transmission). If it's a true stall with prior knock, prioritize oil system integrity — check oil pressure, inspect the oil pressure switch for leaks (flagged in investigation DP21002), and perform a bore scope inspection before any restart attempt. Do not crank an engine with a history of knock-then-stall without establishing oil pressure. Check for NHTSA Campaign 20V519000 and 21V331000 on 2014–2015 models — brake system involvement in some stall events points to ABS/hydraulic recall exposure.

Representative complaint excerpts:
> *"Driving on highway and suddenly heard loud tapping/rattling sound from engine and then engine died resulting in total loss of power steering and acceleration"*
> *"I had catastrophic engine failure while driving with no warning lights indicating my engine was losing oil, the engine and power shut off at 55 miles per hour causing me to lose power steering and power..."*

### Electrical / Instrument Cluster
**62 complaints mention this pattern (31.0%)**

**What it is:** Instrument cluster warnings, erratic gauge behavior, and — critically — the absence of expected warning lights during active mechanical failures. This pattern is tied to both standalone electrical faults and the downstream effects of engine failure events.

**How it presents:** Customers report multiple warning lights illuminating simultaneously with no clear root cause, or conversely, report a complete engine failure with no prior cluster warnings whatsoever. In several documented cases, the oil pressure and engine warning lights never activated despite active oil starvation — suggesting either sensor failure or a cluster communication fault that masked the underlying condition.

**What to look for:** On any Sorento with multiple simultaneous warning lights, do a full module scan — not just engine codes. Check for U-codes indicating lost communication between modules. For 2016 model years, verify recall status under Campaign 22V612000 (transmission lever) and check the multi-function switch for wear per active service campaign SC330; headlight and cluster behavior are directly linked to switch degradation on these vehicles. Oil pressure sender integrity should be verified on any unit with an engine complaint where the customer reports no prior warning — investigation DP21002 specifically flagged oil pressure switch leaks as a known failure mode.

Representative complaint excerpts:
> *"Several unknown warning lights were illuminated"*
> *"I had catastrophic engine failure while driving with no warning lights indicating my engine was losing oil, the engine and power shut off at 55 miles per hour causing me to lose power steering and power..."*
> *"There were no warning signs, no engine indicator light on the dash"*

### Steering
**35 complaints mention this pattern (17.5%)**

**What it is:** Power steering loss, primarily as a consequence of engine stall events — but also documented as a standalone EPAS (electric power assist steering) failure on 2018 models. Two distinct failure modes: secondary loss during engine-off events, and a structural recall affecting the EPAS mounting hardware.

**How it presents:** In engine-failure-adjacent complaints, steering goes heavy simultaneously with the engine dying. As a standalone failure, customers describe progressive loss of power assist or sudden stiffness with no engine issue. DTC codes C1277 through C1938 will be present on EPAS-specific failures.

**What to look for:** On 2018 model years, verify recall status under NHTSA Campaign 18V656000 and Transport Canada 2018533 — the cowl crossbar bracket securing the EPAS column assembly may be improperly welded, creating a structural failure risk. This is a no-charge recall repair that must be confirmed before returning any 2018 Sorento to the road. On non-2018 units with steering complaints, isolate whether the complaint is truly EPAS-originating or secondary to an engine stall. Pull C-codes and cross-reference with the eight steering-specific TSBs on file for this generation. Do not clear steering codes without road testing to confirm full power assist function.

Representative complaint excerpts:
> *"Driving on highway and suddenly heard loud tapping/rattling sound from engine and then engine died resulting in total loss of power steering and acceleration"*
> *"With little to no warning my check engine, steering, and oil lights turned on and the engine stalled after a loud noise as I was coming to a stop"*

### Engine Misfires / Rough Running
**30 complaints mention this pattern (15.0%)**

**What it is:** Misfires and rough idle on the Theta II 2.4L GDI, often accompanied by P0018 (camshaft/crankshaft correlation) and related timing chain or variable valve timing system codes. This is a documented precursor to more severe engine damage if not addressed early.

**How it presents:** Customer reports rough idle, hesitation under load, sluggish low-speed acceleration, or a check engine light with misfire codes. P0018 is specifically called out in complaint narratives and points to timing chain stretch or VVT actuator issues. The vehicle may still drive but performance is degraded and internal wear is ongoing.

**What to look for:** Pull all misfires codes and note which cylinders are affected — random misfires across multiple cylinders on the 2.4L GDI point toward oil delivery issues (VVT solenoid screens, timing chain lubrication). Specific misfires may indicate injector failure; reference active TSB CA2505 covering GDI fuel injector replacement for misfire DTCs on the 2.5L GDI, with parallel logic applicable to the 2.4L. Check VVT solenoid screen condition and oil viscosity. A P0018 on any Theta II should trigger an oil consumption assessment — do not dismiss it as a sensor issue without verifying mechanical integrity. Review TSB SC340 and associated ECM logic improvement campaigns before condemning hardware.

Representative complaint excerpts:
> *"The vehicle developed progressive engine failure symptoms including: Engine knocking and abnormal mechanical noise Loss of power while driving Misfire conditions Excessive oil consumption..."*
> *"My vehicle has a slow speed pick up and I'm getting code P0018 and has very rough idling"*

### HVAC / AC
**24 complaints mention this pattern (12.0%)**

**What it is:** Engine overheating, often mischaracterized initially as an HVAC fault. The 3.3L V6 has a documented internal coolant leak issue that Kia addressed with a warranty extension campaign. Several complaints describe overheating at highway speed leading directly to engine failure.

**How it presents:** Customer may complain of reduced heat output, coolant smell, or the temperature gauge climbing. In more severe cases the vehicle enters a fail-safe mode or the engine fails completely during highway operation. Coolant loss without an obvious external leak is the key indicator.

**What to look for:** On 3.3L V6 units, verify whether the customer received and acted on the 3.3L V6 coolant leak warranty extension notification. Pressure-test the cooling system and check for combustion gas contamination in the coolant (block test) before chasing external leaks. If the customer reports prior overheating events and no corrective action was taken, assess the head gasket and cooling system integrity thoroughly before any engine restart. Cross-reference with the 44 Engine and Engine Cooling TSBs on file — this system has the highest TSB volume of any category in this dataset.

Representative complaint excerpts:
> *"Driving the 2015 Kia Sorento coming back from work. The car overheated and complete engine failure, check engine light with flashing"*
> *"3L V6 Engines Coolant Leak Extension of Warranty and/or Financial reimbursement of a repair done prior to receive this notice"*

### Oil Leaks / Consumption
**23 complaints mention this pattern (11.5%)**

**What it is:** Abnormal oil consumption on the Theta II 2.4L GDI — consuming oil between changes without external leaking, with no dashboard warning until the engine is critically low. This is a well-documented defect that has been the subject of NHTSA investigation (PE23019, DP21002) and multiple TSBs. It is the root-cause mechanism behind many of the catastrophic engine failures in this dataset.

**How it presents:** Customer may report no symptoms until the engine fails. In cases where consumption is caught early, the shop technician or a quick lube facility flags low oil at a routine service interval. Some customers report the oil pressure light appearing just before failure — others report none at all. Oil is being burned, not leaked externally.

**What to look for:** Any 2015–2017 Sorento with the 2.4L engine that comes in for a routine service should have an oil consumption check initiated if not already on file. The NHTSA investigation PE23019 (closed 2023) focused specifically on lubrication system failures and loss of motive power on these engines. If consumption is confirmed, document the rate (quarts per 1,000 miles) and cross-reference with Kia's warranty extension parameters — many of these engines qualify for no-charge replacement or short block assembly if consumption meets the defined threshold.
